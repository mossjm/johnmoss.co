Fourth-Generation Farmer Turned Tech Visionary Builds Empire at the Intersection of Heritage and Innovation

By The Editors · Profile

In the heart of Wisconsin's cranberry country, John Moss is quietly building the future of agriculture — one system at a time, one generation at a time.

At forty years old, John Moss straddles two worlds with uncommon ease. On any given morning, he might review GIS data from a drone survey of his family's cranberry bogs, then pivot to a product meeting for Blossom, the agricultural SaaS platform he founded. By afternoon, he could be fielding calls about autonomous farming equipment as an FJ Dynamics dealer, or preparing for a Wisconsin Cranberry Board session in his capacity as president.

This is not the scattered portfolio of a dilettante. Each venture connects to a central thesis that Moss articulates with the precision of someone who has spent years refining it: build things that last.

"To build things that last — foundations that turn chaos into clarity through systems that reduce friction, create leverage, and leave people more capable than when I found them."

— John Moss

The Elm Lake Cranberry farm — 150 acres of bogs in Wisconsin Rapids — has been in the Moss family for over a century, spanning four generations. But under John's stewardship, the farm has become something more than a family legacy. It is a living laboratory, a testing ground where tradition meets precision agriculture, where the wisdom of generations informs the application of cutting-edge technology.

From this foundation grew Elm Lake Labs, an agricultural technology company that delivers GIS services, drone operations, and serves as a dealer for FJ Dynamics autonomous farming equipment. The company represents Moss's conviction that the future of farming lies not in replacing human knowledge but in augmenting it with intelligent systems.

In 2026, Moss will join the Board of Directors at Ocean Spray, bringing his unique dual perspective as both a grower and a technologist to one of agriculture's most storied cooperatives. It is, perhaps, the most visible validation of an approach that has been quietly building momentum in Wisconsin's agricultural community for years.
